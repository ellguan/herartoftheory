We are the heart of science.




Did you know?

According to the U.S. Department of Labor, while women make up 46.8% of the workforce, women only make up 28% of STEM jobs.

Likewise, only 21% of engineers are women, and only 19% of computer scientists are women.

Why does this happen?

Let's break it down.

1. There is a major underrepresentation of STEM women in the workforce, which means there are less models for younger women to look up to. This discourages young women from working in such fields, which in turn, reinforces the fewer women STEM majors. This becomes an endless cycle.

2. STEM majors, especially those relating to math and physics, are seen as more masculine. This harmful bias prevents younger women from pursuing such fields.

3. Women already in these fields are often mistreated. There have been many cases where professionals and teachers look down and overlook the potential of young women. Similarly, there have been many instances where the work of women scientists has been overshadowed or overlooked. Learn more about these stories here.

What do we do?

Here at Her Art of Theory, our mission is to spread awareness about the significance of supporting young women in working in the math and sciences, so all of us, men and women alike, can bring a better future.

What can you do?

Every big change starts small.

One of the most impactful actions anyone can do is spread the word! Educate yourself and those around you by sharing this website and related resources.

If you are a student, talk to a teacher or school official about creating STEM opportunities around your school. You can also lead a club that advocates for more support and equality among all genders in STEM studies.

If you are a parent, join a parents' association at your student's school and speak out about encouraging everyone, not just one group, to learn about STEM subjects.

Maryam Mirzakhan

Image credit: Wikimedia Commons

This Iranian mathematician grew up in an all girls' school with a principal who didn't anticipate any woman competing for Iran's International Mathematical Olympiad Team, but in both her junior and senior year of high school, Maryam won a gold medal in the competition. She later became the only woman ever to win the Fields medal for her work in hyperbolic surfaces and topology. This medal is comparable to the Nobel Prize for mathematics. In her honor, the University of Oxford created the Mirzhakani Society for women and non-binary students studying mathematics.

Chien-Shiung Wu

Image credit: Smithsonian Institution Archives

Nicknamed "The First Lady of Physics", this Chinese-American nuclear physicist joined the Manhattan Project, a top-secret project to build the first nuclear bomb in Colombia University in 1944. After the war, her work turned towards beta decay, when she conducted an experiment proving that the law of parity does not hold up during beta decay. This experiment is now known as the Wu Experiment, however, instead of Wu winning the Nobel Prize for her work, two male fellow physicists who approached her into conducting this experiment won the famed award instead. Later in her life, she continued her research that later shifted to sickle-cell anemia and was a strong advocate for young girls in STEM to pursue their interests.

Barbara McClintock

Image credit: Smithsonian Institution Archives

This American biologist first found a deep interest in science at a very young age, when she studied that major at Cornell University in 1919. At first, her mother would not let her go, because her mother's first priority was for her to get married. However, she did anyway, and this eventually led to her studying the chromosomal makeup of corn. Genetics, her field of study, was way ahead of her time and was ignored for many years, until the 70's, when the scientific community finally recognized her. She was the first woman to be the sole winner of the 1983 Nobel Prize for Physiology or Medicine.
